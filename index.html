Unforgettable Experience (Travel Agency)

Deposit Policy Impact Analysis

Data pipeline & validation · Natural experiment design · Diagnostic analysis

Context

In August 2025, a small Guadalajara travel agency raised its required booking deposit from 20% to 50% non-refundable, aiming to stop customers from cancelling.

The question

Did the deposit increase actually reduce cancellations, or did it quietly drive away the agency's most valuable customers?

Approach

  • Cleaned 388 real bookings from a genuinely messy source (6 date formats, 5 money formats, duplicate records, and impossible values) using a PostgreSQL pipeline
  • Built a Python validation layer to quantify cleaning accuracy and profile remaining data gaps, rather than assume the clean data was correct
  • Framed the policy change as a natural experiment (pre/post comparison), explicitly testing and reporting its limits rather than overclaiming causality
  • Designed a two-page interactive Power BI dashboard, deliberately choosing native visuals, custom HTML/DAX, or Deneb/Vega-Lite per chart based on what each one actually required

Outcome

  • Raising the deposit worked on the symptom and created a new problem
  • Cancellations fell 57% (19.4% → 8.3%), and refunds from cancelled bookings dropped to zero after the change
  • But the same 50%-upfront rule suppressed the agency's highest-margin segment: average monthly custom-trip bookings fell from 12.4 to 9.0 (−27%), even though these trips (weddings, quinceañeras, honeymoons) are worth 2.1× a group trip on average

Key findings

1. Cancellations Fell 57% Overnight

Raising the deposit from 20% to 50% cut the cancellation rate from 19.4% to 8.3% almost immediately. On paper, the policy did exactly what it was designed to do.

2. The Real Leak Wasn't Discounts

Of the $73.4M booked, $12.8M was lost to cancellations (12x more than was lost to last-minute discounts). The obvious cost was never the real problem.

3. The Best Customers Were Pushed Away

Custom trips fell 27% a month after the change, even though they're worth 2.1× a group booking. The fix for one problem quietly created another.

Recommendations

  • 1. Keep the 50% deposit for group trips.
    The policy works here without suppressing volume.
  • 2. Flexible deposit for custom trips, by trip type.
    Offer 20–30% upfront plus a structured payment plan (e.g. a second installment at 90 days before departure) specifically for weddings, XV años, and honeymoons, regardless of how far ahead they're booked. This recovers the premium customers the flat 50% rule deters, while the staged second payment preserves commitment.
  • 3. Add a checkpoint for long-lead bookings, by timing.
    Separately, for any trip type booked more than 180 days out, require a second deposit at the 90-day mark. This targets the highest-cancellation cohort (32%) based on how early it was booked, not what kind of trip it is.
  • 4. Protect channel quality.
    Web bookings cancel at roughly half the rate of travel-fair leads. Directing more demand to the site, and treating travel-fair leads with more caution (deposits, follow-up), improves the quality of the overall booking portfolio.

F2P LiveOps

Game Health Check

Acquisition Analysis· Retention Analysis · Monetization Analysis

Context

A free-to-play mobile game needed a health check across acquisition, retention, and monetization, but the underlying data (players, sessions, purchases, live events, marketing spend) arrived messy: inconsistent date formats, duplicate and test accounts, mixed currencies, and unlabeled quality issues, mirroring the real state of LiveOps telemetry. (Synthetic dataset, deliberately built with these conditions as real F2P transaction data isn't publicly available.)

The question

Where in the player lifecycle (acquisition, retention, or monetization) is the game losing players or money, and what should be fixed first?

Approach

  • Audited and cleaned five raw data sources (~9,000 players, ~79,000 sessions, ~3,500 purchases, marketing spend, and event logs) in PostgreSQL, resolving 12 distinct data-quality issues (inconsistent formats, duplicates, test records, currency mismatches)
  • Wrote SQL to answer five business questions: churn drivers, marketing ROAS, revenue concentration, refund leakage, and install-reporting accuracy
  • Built a 3-section Tableau dashboard (Acquisition → Retention → Monetization) with KPI callouts and a consistent color system flagging problem areas

Outcome

  • Isolated a single live event responsible for nearly double the normal churn rate
  • Identified one marketing channel returning €0.17 per €1 spent, while others broke even or profited
  • Quantified a specific, recoverable revenue leak tied to one product
  • Quantified how concentrated the player base is: the top 10% of payers drive nearly a third of all revenue
  • Uncovered a structural install-attribution gap affecting every paid channel, not just one bad platform

Key findings

Event-driven churn

Forge Frenzy churned 57.9% of active players: roughly 2x any other event. Pointing to a difficulty spike specific to this event, not competitive events in general.

Marketing efficiency

Influencer marketing returned €0.17 per €1 spent, the only channel below break-even; cross_promo returned €2.73.

Revenue concentration

Two offer types drive 52% of revenue, and the top 30% of paying players account for two-thirds of it, with only 13.2% of players converting to payers at all.

Payer concentration (by decile)

The top decile of paying players alone contributes 32.7% of total revenue; by the top half of payers, that reaches 83.5%, a steep whale curve typical of F2P economies, and a signal for where retention effort pays back the most.

Revenue leakage

gems_mega refunds ran at 14.7% versus 0% for every other offer, €1,300 in recoverable revenue tied to a single product.

Attribution accuracy

Every paid acquisition channel over-reported installs by 10–13.5% versus actual, a structural attribution gap, not one bad platform. Shown here is the query that surfaces it: a join between platform-reported install logs and verified actual installs, aggregated by channel.

Recommendations

  • 1. Rebalance Forge Frenzy's difficulty curve before its next run. The churn spike is event-specific, not category-wide
  • 2. Reallocate influencer budget toward cross_promo and google_uac; track ROAS by channel monthly
  • 3. Protect the top-30%-payer segment with retention/VIP treatment, since it drives two-thirds of revenue, losing a handful of these players costs more than losing hundreds of non-payers
  • 4. Investigate the gems_mega purchase flow specifically (not gems generally) to recover the €1,300 in refund leakage
  • 5. Reconcile reported-vs-actual installs with ad partners before setting CAC targets, so acquisition cost isn't calculated on inflated numbers

E-commerce Marketplace

Seller Funnel & Buyer Retention Analysis

Funnel Analysis · Cohort & Retention Analysis · Data Quality Testing

Context

Olist is a real Brazilian marketplace connecting ~3,000 third-party sellers to buyers across multiple sales channels, structurally similar to how Zalando or Amazon's marketplace programs work. This analysis combines two real datasets Olist itself released: ~100,000 e-commerce orders and 8,000 seller-acquisition leads, to look at both sides of the marketplace at once.

The question

Which lead-acquisition channels and which kind of seller actually convert and succeed on the platform, and once a customer buys once, how likely are they to come back, and does what they bought first matter?

Approach

  • Built a 9-table normalized PostgreSQL schema joining two separately-released real datasets via seller_id, modeling a full 3-stage funnel (lead → closed deal → activated seller)
  • Wrote a 9-check automated data-quality suite, catching and documenting two real pipeline bugs along the way rather than treating "it ran" as "it's correct"
  • Used Python (pandas, scipy) for chi-square/Cramér's V significance testing on every headline claim, and power-law vs. exponential curve fitting for the retention decay

Outcome

  • Seller type beats channel as a predictor of success: resellers activate at 48.89% vs. 37.19% for manufacturers.
  • Conversion rate alone hides the real problem: 54.9% of closed deals never resulted in a seller who made a single sale
  • What a customer buys first predicts whether they come back: home_appliances customers repeat at 9.14%, roughly 3x the 3.05% baseline for every other category
  • Data quality check/test discovered a dataset trap: grouping by the wrong customer-id column shows 0% repeat purchases; the correct column shows 3.12%

Key findings

Seller Type Beats Channel

Resellers activate at 48.89% vs. 37.19% for manufacturers (p=0.002). Business segment matters even more: household_utilities and pet sellers activate 2x better than food or computers sellers.

The Funnel Has 3 Stages, Not 2

Conversion rate alone misses that 54.9% of closed deals (462 of 842) never resulted in a seller who made a single sale. Evaluating a channel on lead-to-deal conversion alone would have missed this entirely.

No Single Channel Metric Tells the Whole Story

direct_traffic looks unremarkable on lead-to-deal conversion (11.22%) but has the fastest median close (10.6 days) and highest activation rate (55.36%) of any channel.

What You Buy First Predicts If You'll Return

Customers whose first purchase was home_appliances repeat at 9.14%, roughly 3x the site-wide baseline (p<0.001). Electronics and consoles_games are genuinely one-and-done categories.

Repeat-Purchase Is Rare and Front-Loaded

Monthly retention follows a power-law decay (R² = 0.78 vs. 0.59 for exponential). Only 0.48% return in month 1, dropping to 0.30% by month 2, essentially all repeat-purchase signal happens fast or not at all.

The customer_id Trap

Grouping repeat customers by the wrong ID column shows exactly 0% ever return, by construction. The correct person-level column shows 3.12%, a documented trap in the dataset's own dictionary, demonstrated live rather than just quietly avoided.

Recommendations

  • 1. Recruit resellers, not manufacturers.
    Target segments like household_utilities, pet, and sports_leisure specifically.
  • 2. Feature home_appliances and fashion_bags_accessories to new customers.
    These categories build a repeat customer base at 1.5–3x the site-wide rate, a merchandising decision, not a tracking fix.
  • 3. Scale direct_traffic and paid_search.
    Both are genuinely strong across all 3 funnel stages, unlike raw lead volume alone would suggest.
  • 4. Refresh social's targeting and creative channel-wide.
    Its weak conversion isn't concentrated in one landing page (checked directly, not statistically significant); the fix has to be channel-level, not a single-page swap.
  • 5. Deprioritize email and other_publicities.
    Weak at every funnel stage and the lowest realized revenue of any channel tested.
  • 6. Front-load re-engagement to the first 30 days.
    Essentially all repeat-purchase signal happens by month 1, regardless of category.

Machine Learning Operations

Adaptive Model Risk Monitoring

An end-to-end case study in statistical modeling, feature engineering, and production model monitoring, using professional esports data (League of Legends).

Statistical Modeling · Calibration · Drift Detection · Production Monitoring

Context

I started this project to get real practice with statistical modelling, ETL pipelines, and production-style monitoring, the parts that get skipped once most tutorials manage to get a model to train. League of Legends, a game I've followed for years, turned out to be a strong sandbox: Oracle's Elixir publishes free, detailed match history, and the patch cycle gives a genuine, dateable point where the underlying process changes, exactly what a monitoring system needs to be tested against. What follows evaluates a win-probability model the way a deployed one would be evaluated: calibration testing, drift detection, variance analysis across a real regime change, and an automated rule flagging when performance needs review. Brier score improves from 0.230 to 0.116 as a match progresses, with calibration holding at every stage.

What's under the hood

  • Pregame win-probability model (logistic regression, chronological train/test split, leakage checked line by line)
  • In-play model re-scoring the same games at 10/15/20/25-minute checkpoints
  • Drift detection anchored to a real patch boundary, not a calendar date
  • An automated retrain-recommendation rule, walked forward patch by patch
  • A per-prediction confidence/reliability flag, validated against real outcomes

Distinguishing Drift From a False Alarm

A monitoring metric spiked to 1.75, roughly seven times the usual threshold for a material shift. The cause wasn't a change in the underlying data: that specific feature resets at the start of every patch by construction, so its distribution is guaranteed to look unstable across any patch boundary regardless of whether conditions actually changed. Confirming that before treating the spike as real drift avoided a false retraining trigger.

How I built it

Feature Engineering

The .shift(1) is the whole point. Without it, a team's rolling win rate would include the very game the model is trying to predict. Every feature in this project was checked against this same rule before it was allowed near the model.

Drift Detection

This ran clean and returned 1.75, about 7x the usual "materially shifted" threshold. It wasn't real drift: that specific feature resets every patch by construction, so it's guaranteed to look unstable regardless of whether the world changed. The math was right; the question it was pointed at wasn't.

Monitoring

One patch got flagged "monitor closely" on input drift alone, even though the model performed better than baseline there. Drift and performance don't always move together; a rule that only watched one of them would have missed that.

Reliability

A confident-looking 0.82 built on 3 games of history isn't as trustworthy as an equally confident 0.82 built on 10, even though the model reports them identically. This flag separates the two, and was validated against real outcomes rather than just asserted.

If This Were Production

  • 1. Weight the in-play model more heavily as the match progresses.
    Its discrimination is substantially stronger than the pregame model's; treat the pregame model as a starting point only, before more information becomes available.
  • 2. Audit any drift feature for construction-based instability before wiring it to an alert.
    A metric can be computed correctly and still be pointed at the wrong thing, as the PSI false alarm above shows directly.
  • 3. Treat "monitor closely" as a real third state, not a binary switch.
    Route it to human review instead of forcing every patch into retrain-or-ignore.
